Reusing it here means DCA inherits the calibrator-selection optimism — negligible, since selection was a two-way near-tie (ECE 0.0264 vs 0.0272). precision@k / recall@k are RANKING-based and calibration-invariant, so they carry ZERO selection optimism. The model never trained on S_eval. No cleaner internal surface remains; the honest move is reuse-with-documentation, not re-fragmenting the data.

## Decision-curve analysis (net benefit)

DCA is computed on **calibrated** probabilities — the threshold axis `p_t` is an expected-utility (odds) axis and is only meaningful on calibrated probs.

The calibrated model **weakly dominates (≥)** both treat-all and treat-none at every threshold on the evaluated grid [0.01, 0.50], and **strictly dominates across most of it**. At the lowest thresholds it converges to treat-all (both flag nearly everyone). Its net benefit then decays toward — but does not reach — the treat-none line within the grid: a minority of patients carry high calibrated risk (the isotonic output ceiling is 1.00), so NB_model stays marginally positive (0.0010) even at p_t=0.50, and would tie treat-none only above the maximum calibrated risk.

That NB_model never drops below treat-none (0) at any threshold is a direct consequence of **calibration**: NB_model ≥ 0 exactly when the flagged group's precision ≥ `p_t`, and for a calibrated model the flagged-group precision tracks its mean predicted risk, which is ≥ `p_t` by construction (only patients with `p_cal ≥ p_t` are flagged).

Treat-all goes **negative for p_t > prevalence (0.1145)**, so beating it there is trivial. The clinically meaningful margin sits at thresholds at or below prevalence (**p_t ≈ 0.05–0.15**), where net benefit exceeds treat-all by **~+0.035 on average** (from +0.003 at 0.05 to +0.074 at 0.15) — on the order of a few extra true positives per 100 patients at no added false-positive cost. (The earlier 'beats across the entire grid' framing overstated this — it conflated weak with strict dominance and credited the trivial beat of a negative treat-all.)

## Operating points (fixed budgets, no data-snooping)

Budgets are pre-declared care-team capacities (5/10/20% of the worklist), not the best-looking points. CIs are patient-grouped bootstrap (B=1000, seed=42).

| budget | alerts/100 | precision (95% CI) | recall (95% CI) | lift | NNF | implied p_t |
|---:|---:|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 5% | 5 | 0.408 [0.367, 0.452] | 0.178 [0.163, 0.194] | 3.56 | 2.5 | 0.2471 |
| 10% | 10 | 0.360 [0.328, 0.394] | 0.314 [0.294, 0.336] | 3.14 | 2.8 | 0.1875 |
| 20% | 20 | 0.280 [0.260, 0.300] | 0.488 [0.465, 0.509] | 2.44 | 3.6 | 0.1604 |

The implied probability threshold connects the top-k worklist view to the DCA threshold axis: flagging the top-k% is equivalent to acting above that calibrated risk.

## Calibration invariance

precision@k computed on the calibrated ordering equals that on the raw @phase1 ordering to within **0.0e+00** across the whole k-grid (isotonic is monotone → identical ranking). In one line: **calibration changes displayed confidence and DCA, NOT who is on the worklist.** Ties in calibrated probability (isotonic's flat regions) are broken by the underlying raw score, which is why the worklist is invariant.

## Honest framing

Discrimination is modest (~0.67 AUROC), which caps achievable recall at low alert rates: flagging the top 10% of encounters captures **31.4%** of 30-day readmissions, the top 20% captures **48.8%**. These are real, un-dressed numbers — useful for triage relative to treat-all/treat-none over the band above, but no substitute for the discrimination ceiling. The cleanest performance number will come from an external validation set in Phase 8, not from this internal surface.
